For arbitrary topological space we have $\overline{\operatorname{int}(\overline{U})} \subseteq \overline{U}$. Since $\operatorname{int}(\overline{U}) \subseteq \overline{U}$ and $\overline{U}$ is closed, it contains the closure of $\operatorname{int}(\overline{U})$.

Ex.4

Pf.1

Let $x \in A \cap \overline{B} \neq \varnothing$. Since $A$ open, there exists an open nbhd $U \subseteq A$ of $x$. Note that $X\setminus A$ is closed and contains $B$. Which means $x \notin \overline{B}$ so $x \notin A \cap \overline{B}$. Contradiction.

Pf. 2

Since $A \cap B = \varnothing$ and $\overline{B} = B \cup B^{\prime}$, we have $A \cap \overline{B} = \varnothing \iff A \cap B^\prime = \varnothing$. Suppose $x \in A$ since $A$ is open, there exists an open nbhd $U \subseteq A$ of $x$, s.t. $U \cap B = \varnothing$. But that means that $x \notin B^\prime$. So $A \cap B^\prime = \varnothing$.
